Sort

Sort — To put things in order, like smallest to largest, A to Z, or shortest to tallest.
Example: When you line up by height in P.E. class, your teacher is sorting you — usually shortest to tallest!

How It Works — Bubble Sort

Bubble sort is one of the simplest sorting algorithms. Here are the rules:

  1. Look at the first TWO items
  2. Are they in the wrong order? SWAP them.
  3. Move to the NEXT pair
  4. Repeat until you reach the end — that's one PASS
  5. Keep doing passes until nothing swaps. Now it's sorted!
Worked Example — Sort: [3] [1] [4] [2]
Start:
3
1
4
2

Pass 1: 3↔1 SWAP, 3↔4 keep, 4↔2 SWAP → [1, 3, 2, 4]

Pass 2: 1↔3 keep, 3↔2 SWAP, 3↔4 keep → [1, 2, 3, 4]

Pass 3: Check all pairs → no swaps needed → DONE!

Notice — the biggest number (4) BUBBLED UP to the end first. That's why it's called bubble sort!

PART B — Why Does Bubble Sort Need NESTED Loops?

Bubble sort has TWO loops working together — the OUTER loop counts passes, and the INNER loop compares pairs within a pass.

Teacher / Parent Note

This is the first named algorithm in the workbook — and it uses every concept your child has learned so far: sequence (Activity 2), loops (Activities 3-4), nested loops (Activity 4), conditionals (Activity 5), and even debugging (Activity 7) when sorts go wrong. Bubble sort isn't the fastest sorting algorithm — but it's the most intuitive, which is why nearly every CS course on Earth starts here. Discussion prompt: Ask your child to spot something in real life that gets sorted regularly (a library shelf, a hand of cards, a classroom by height) and talk through what algorithm might be at work.
