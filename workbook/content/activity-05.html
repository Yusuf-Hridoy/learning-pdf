You've taught a program to repeat steps with loops. But how does a program decide what to do next? With Yes/No questions called conditionals — the same way you decide whether to grab an umbrella in the morning!

Conditional

Conditional — A Yes/No question that decides what happens next. If the answer is Yes, do one thing. If No, do another.
Example: "IF it's raining THEN bring an umbrella OTHERWISE wear sunglasses." That's a conditional you make every morning without even thinking about it!

Challenge Version
PART A — Write the Conditional in Shorthand

Format: IF [condition] THEN [action] OTHERWISE [other action].

Example: A microwave that beeps when food is hot → IF the food is hot THEN beep OTHERWISE keep heating.
Scenario A1
A vending machine that gives you a snack if you put in $2 (otherwise it returns your money)
Scenario A2
A school bell — students go inside if it's 8:00 AM (otherwise they keep playing)
Scenario A3
A library book — you can borrow it if you have a library card (otherwise the librarian asks you to sign up)
PART B — Draw a Flowchart
Scenario B
Decide whether to wear a jacket based on the weather. If the temperature is below 15°C, wear a jacket. Otherwise, leave it at home.
Draw your flowchart here — use a diamond for the question, arrows for Yes/No, and boxes for the actions.

Teacher / Parent Note

This activity introduces conditionals (also called selection or branching) — the third core control structure in programming, completing the trio of sequence, iteration, and selection. Kids learn that programs make decisions using Yes/No questions, just like humans do dozens of times a day. Discussion prompt: Ask your child to spot 3 conditionals their parents or teachers used today (e.g., "If you finish your dinner, you can have dessert").
